Diamond Pricing Analysis

Exploring which physical characteristics — carat, cut, color, and clarity — most strongly influence diamond prices, using a real-world dataset of 53,940 diamonds.

📊 Python · Pandas · Matplotlib 📁 53,940 records 👤 Shireen Zibari
Methodology
  • Loaded and inspected a public diamonds dataset (53,940 rows, 10 features — no missing values)
  • Computed summary statistics and correlations between price and physical attributes
  • Grouped and aggregated prices by categorical grades (cut quality, color grade)
  • Visualized distributions and relationships using Matplotlib

Price Distribution
Insight: Prices are heavily right-skewed — most diamonds cluster under $5,000, with a long tail of expensive outliers, so the median ($2,401) is a more representative "typical price" than the average ($3,933).
Price vs. Carat Weight
Insight: Carat weight is by far the strongest single predictor of price (correlation = 0.92). The relationship is non-linear — price accelerates faster than carat weight increases, especially past the 1-carat mark.
Average Price by Cut Quality
Insight: Counter-intuitively, "Premium" cut diamonds average a higher price than "Ideal" cut ones. This isn't because Premium is objectively better — it reflects that larger, pricier stones in this dataset are more often cut as Premium rather than Ideal.
Average Price by Color Grade
Insight: Grade J (the most tinted/lowest color grade in this dataset) shows the highest average price, while grade E is among the lowest. This mirrors the cut finding — it reflects a mix effect (larger stones happen to fall into lower color grades more often) rather than color alone driving price up.
Feature Correlation
Insight: Price correlates strongly with carat (0.92) and the physical dimensions x, y, z (which are themselves derived from carat) — confirming that size, not cut or color, is the dominant price driver in this dataset.

Conclusion

Carat weight is the dominant factor in diamond pricing (correlation of 0.92 with price), far outweighing cut quality or color grade individually. This suggests that pricing models for this kind of data should prioritize size-based features, while categorical grades like cut and color act more as secondary, interacting factors rather than independent price drivers.
